# Workable Voice & Tone Guide

## Voice vs. Tone

**Voice** is Workable's consistent personality — it never changes regardless of context.  
**Tone** adapts to the situation, the user's emotional state, and the moment in their journey.

---

## Workable's Voice

Workable's voice is defined by three paired attributes. Each pair holds both ends in balance — neither extreme is right on its own.

| Attribute | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Empathetic yet Professional** | We understand users' challenges, but we stay focused and don't over-dramatize |
| **Innovative yet Practical** | We're forward-thinking, but we stay grounded in what actually helps users get work done |
| **Transparent yet Concise** | We're open and honest, but we don't over-explain or add noise |

---

## Writing Values

Every piece of copy should be:

**Simple** — Use everyday language. Avoid jargon, buzzwords, and unnecessary complexity. If a 12-year-old wouldn't understand it, simplify it.

**Direct** — Say what you mean. Don't bury the key message. Get to the point.

**Efficient** — Respect users' time. Every word should earn its place. If a word doesn't add meaning, remove it.

---

## 7 Writing Principles

1. **Shorten and prioritize** — Remove filler words. Put the most important information first.
2. **Focus on actions** — Tell users what they can do, not what the system is doing.
3. **Sound simple** — Use plain, conversational language over formal or technical terms.
4. **Write how you speak** — If you wouldn't say it out loud, don't write it. Read your copy aloud as a test.
5. **Be positive** — Frame things in terms of what users can do, not what they can't.
6. **Use active voice** — "You can add a candidate" not "A candidate can be added."
7. **Support with visuals** — Copy and UI visuals work together. Don't repeat what the interface already communicates.

---

## Tone by Context

Tone shifts based on where the user is and how they're feeling. Use this table as a guide.

| Context | Tone | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Onboarding / first use | Warm, encouraging, guiding | Users are new. Reduce anxiety. Build confidence. |
| In-product (routine tasks) | Clear, efficient, neutral | Users know what they're doing. Get out of their way. |
| Errors / problems | Calm, supportive, helpful | Never blame the user. Focus on the fix, not the failure. |
| Success moments | Positive, brief, human | Celebrate briefly without over-the-top enthusiasm. |
| High-stakes decisions | Careful, precise, transparent | Users need to trust the information. Be accurate above all else. |

---

## Where to Show Personality vs. Where Clarity Wins

**Show personality here:**
- Onboarding flows
- Empty states
- Tooltips and helper text
- Success messages and confirmation screens

**Prioritize clarity here:**
- Error messages
- Flash / alert messages
- Form labels and CTAs
- Any moment where a user needs to take urgent action

---

## Planning Your Writing

Before writing any piece of UI copy, answer three questions:

1. **Who is reading this?** Think about the user — their role, their context, their current emotional state. Are they in the middle of a task? Seeing this for the first time? Recovering from an error?
2. **What do I need them to understand or do?** One message per piece of copy. If there are two messages, consider whether they belong together.
3. **Where will they read this?** A notification mid-task requires different writing than an onboarding welcome screen.

### Writing Aims
Every piece of copy should achieve at least one of these:
- **Inform** — Give the user the information they need
- **Guide** — Help the user take the right action
- **Reassure** — Reduce friction, uncertainty, or anxiety

### Timing Matters
A well-timed message lands differently than the same message shown at the wrong moment. Ask: is this the right moment to show this? Would the user find this helpful right now, or would it feel intrusive?

---

## The Final Rule

**If a word doesn't add meaning, remove it.**

Every word is a cognitive cost. Shorter is almost always better.
